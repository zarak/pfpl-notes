2 Inference Rules

  • An inductive definition of a judgment form consists of a collection of rules of the form

    \begin{equation} \frac{J_1 ~ \cdots ~ J_k}{J} \end{equation}

    in which \(J\) and \(J_1, \cdots, J_k\) are all judgments of the form being defined.

  • The judgments above the horizontal line are called the premises of the rule, and the judgment below the line is called its conclusion.
  • If a rule has no premises, the rule is called an axiom; otherwise it is called a proper rule.
  • An inference rule can be read as stating that the premises are sufficient for the conclusion: to show \(J\), it is enough to show \(J_1, \cdots, J_k\).
  • When \(k\) is \(0\), a rule states that its conclusion holds unconditionally.
  • Bear in mind that there may be, in general, many rules with the same conclusion, each specifying sufficient conditions for the conclusion. Consequently, if the conclusion of a rule holds, then it is not necessary that the premises hold, for it might have been derived by another rule.

  • Example 1

    \begin{align*} \frac{~}{\text{ zero nat }} \\ \\ \frac{a \text{ nat}}{\text{ zero nat }} \end{align*}

    Thus \(a\) nat holds whenever \(a\) is zero or \(a\) is \(\text{succ}(b)\) where \(b\) nat for some \(b\).

  • Example 2 The judgment form \(a\) is \(b\) expresses the equality of two abt’s \(a\) and \(b\) such that \(a\) nat and \(b\) nat is inductively defined by the following rules:

    \begin{align*} \frac{~}{\text{ zero is zero }} \\ \\ \frac{a \text{ is } b}{\text{succ a is succ b}} \end{align*}

3 Derivations

  • To show that an inductively defined judgment holds, it is enough to exhibit a derivation of it.

  • If

    \begin{equation} \frac{J_1 ~ \cdots ~ J_k}{J} \end{equation}

    is an inference rule and \(\nabla_1, \cdots, \nabla_k\) are derivations of its permises, then

    \begin{equation} \frac{\nabla_1 ~ \cdots ~ \nabla_k}{J} \end{equation}

    is a derivation of its conclusion.

  • Example Here is a derivation of node(node(empty; empty); empty) tree:

    \begin{equation} \overline{\text{empty tree}} ~~ \overline{\text{empty tree}} \\ \overline{\text {node(empty;empty) tree}} ~~ \overline{\text{empty tree}} \\ \overline{\text {node(node(empty;empty);empty) tree}} \end{equation}
  • There are two main methods for finding derivations
    • forward chaining or bottom-up construciton
    • backward chaining or top-down construction

4 Rule Induction

  • The property \(\mathcal P\) respects the rule

    \[ \frac{a_1 ~ \mathsf J ~~ \cdots ~~ a_k ~ \mathsf J}{a ~ \mathsf J} \]

    if \(\mathcal P(a)\) holds whenever \(P(a_1), \cdots, P(a_k)\) do. The assumptions \(\mathcal P(a_1), \cdots, \mathcal P(a_k)\) are called the inductive hypotheses, and \(\mathcal P(a)\) is called the inductive conclusion of the inference.
